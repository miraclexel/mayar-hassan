Project:

India Air Pollution

A project to answer the following question:

How can we reduce air pollution in New Delhi?

Skills / tools Used

Figma
Design Thinking
Behavioural Science
Stakeholder Interviews
multidiciplinary working
Solution Pitching

Figma was primarily used as a tool for brainstorming among the group and managing the project

Behavioural science was used to decompose the wicked problem that was New Delhi's air pollution and to try to find solutions that would work.

Under my guidance, the group was following a design thinking approach to solving the problem, and as such, it was important to understand our stakeholders, including through interviews.

The project was made up of students across engineering. We had to take a multidiciplinary approach to the project.

Our final deliverable was a report to pitch our potential solution. Our report received a first class.

My Role

Project manager, UX designer

My Contribution

I led the team of 6 engineers through a design thinking process in order to find an innovative solution to the wicked problem of air pollution in New Delhi.


What Was the Challenge?

Coming up with an innovative solution to a wicked problem while managing engineers who had no previous familiarity with design thinking and whose schedules often clashed.

Final Product

A fully costed proposed innovative solution that tackled the root cause of the problem.

This program took place full time over two weeks remotely, unsupervised, as a marked project by the department of engineering. However, many of the team had clashing responsibilities, meaning that very few could make it for the entire time neccesary. This meant that I had to design tasks in a way that they could be done asynchronously. A figma board was used for this purpose. The first step was to research as much as possible about the problem. While secondary research is important in laying the groundwork for understanding the problem, primary research - especially observation, user diaries and suplementary in-depth interviews - are crucial to human-centred design. However, since we did not have access to any of these methods, we substituted primary research with watching videos about farming in India as a kind of psudo-observation. Everything that every person on the team found was put on a mind map to allow for visual links to be made between ideas


Our research period allowed us to then condense the problem. This was vital not just for external project assesors, but also for the team itself to have a clear understanding of the problem we were working to solve


This secondary research allowed us to note down all stakeholder along with their needs and concerns


We found existing solutions and discussed why they weren't effective at solving the problem. I ensured that all team members put forward any questions they had when we're evaulating. I've found previously that the times in teams when we've done worse together than we would have individually, it's often because we have not aired these 'questions'.


I assigned each person in the team a different way of consolodating the information we'd learned in the define stage.


From the work done, we were able to make a mind map of problem statements.


while design thinking likes to keep from jumping at solutions too early in the process, I know that inspiration can jump at any point. As such a mind map was available throughout the process to place any ideas down. At the end of our define stage, we also spend a couple of minutes together brainstorming about any obvious solutions that we'd already been thinking about.


A more formalised process of ideation followed. brainwriting is one of my preferred forms of ideation as it forces a large quantity of ideas to be generated that all focus on answering specific problem statements.

In order to brainwrite, I first wrote the name of each member of our team down in both column and row names to create a Matrix. I asked each team member to choose a problem statement (that must be unique to others chosen) and write it under their name. I then gave everyone a day to go through and write a possible solution to everyone's problem statement. The solution must be unique from everyone else's solution to that problem. This task is designed to be done under timed conditions in real time, with team members rotating through others' ideas with 2 minutes to get down a solution to each problem statement. However, having recently redone this excersise in real time on a seperate project, I found that it was more successful when team members could find solutions in their own time. One downside, however, as can be seen in the image is that sometimes team members will do their part after everyone else has already finished and the team moved on. This was, however, unique since that particular team member had been flying.


We took a look at all the solutions that we came up with in brain writing and grouped and evaluated them.

Our final solution was a system wide solution, which can be found in the presentation at the start of this section.
